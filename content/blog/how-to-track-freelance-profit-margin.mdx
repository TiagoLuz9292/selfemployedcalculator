Revenue tells you how busy you are. Profit margin tells you if the business is actually working. Many freelancers celebrate a $10,000 month without asking what it cost them to deliver — and are surprised when there's little left over.

Here's how to track profit margin properly.

## Two Margins Every Freelancer Should Know

**Gross Profit Margin**: revenue minus direct project costs.

> Gross Margin = (Revenue - Direct Costs) ÷ Revenue × 100

Direct costs are expenses tied directly to delivering client work: subcontractor fees, project-specific software, stock assets, client entertainment.

**Net Profit Margin**: revenue minus all costs — direct costs plus operating overhead.

> Net Margin = (Revenue - All Costs) ÷ Revenue × 100

Operating overhead includes software subscriptions, insurance, accounting fees, equipment amortization, marketing costs, and everything else.

Use the [profit margin calculator](/calculators/profit-margin-calculator) to calculate both instantly.

## Healthy Benchmarks

Because freelancers have lower overhead than product businesses, healthy margins are higher:

| Net Margin | Assessment |
|------------|-----------|
| Below 20% | Concerning |
| 20-35% | Average for service businesses |
| 35-50% | Healthy — most solo freelancers target this |
| 50-65% | Excellent |
| Above 65% | Outstanding — typically high-specialization consulting |

Solo freelancers with minimal overhead (no subcontractors, low fixed costs) should be achieving 45-65% net margins. If you're well below this, expenses are too high, rates are too low, or both.

## How to Track It Monthly

**Set up a simple P&L**. Every month, record:

```
Revenue:                $[X]
− Direct costs:         $[Y]
= Gross profit:         $[Z]
  Gross margin:         Z/X × 100 = [%]

− Operating expenses:   $[A]
= Net profit:           $[B]
  Net margin:           B/X × 100 = [%]
```

Do this in a spreadsheet or use accounting software that generates it automatically. Most invoicing tools (FreshBooks, Wave, QuickBooks) produce a monthly P&L with one click.

**Track the trend, not just the number**. A single month's margin is a data point. Three months of declining margins is a warning signal.

## Why Your Margin Might Be Shrinking

**Rate stagnation**: if your expenses grow with inflation but your rates don't, margin shrinks automatically. Review rates annually.

**Scope creep**: work you do but don't bill for. Track actual hours on every project to identify where unbilled time is accumulating.

**Increased subcontractor use**: outsourcing reduces your margin. If you're regularly subcontracting at a thin spread, re-evaluate whether that work model is sustainable.

**Subscription creep**: software subscriptions accumulate. Do a quarterly audit of everything you're paying for and cancel anything you're not using actively.

**Low-margin clients**: some clients generate lower revenue per hour than others due to high non-billable time. The [client profitability calculator](/calculators/client-profitability-calculator) can identify which clients are pulling your margin down.

## The Fastest Way to Improve Margin

**Raise rates**: a 10% rate increase on existing work costs nothing to deliver and goes entirely to profit. A freelancer billing $80,000 at 40% margin earns $32,000. The same freelancer billing $88,000 at the same cost base earns $40,000 — a 25% increase in profit from a 10% rate increase.

**Eliminate non-essential expenses**: audit every subscription annually. Cancel anything you use less than weekly. The SaaS industry is designed to keep you subscribed — resist.

**Stop absorbing scope creep**: every unreported revision, extra call, or additional deliverable is margin destruction. Use change orders for anything outside the original scope.

**Productize services**: offering standardized service packages instead of fully custom work reduces scope variability and improves estimation — both of which protect margins.

## Monthly Profit Margin Reporting

Set a recurring calendar reminder on the first week of each month to review last month's P&L. Ask:

1. What was my net margin?
2. Is this higher or lower than the previous month?
3. What drove any significant change?
4. Are there any expense categories that grew unexpectedly?

This monthly habit takes 15-20 minutes and ensures you catch margin compression before it becomes a crisis.
